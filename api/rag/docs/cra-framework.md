# CRA Framework Overview for Fraction Instruction

The Concrete-Representational-Abstract (CRA) sequence, rooted in Bruner's (1966) enactive-iconic-symbolic theory, is the strongest evidence-based instructional progression for teaching fractions. Each stage builds on the prior one; skipping stages is a primary source of procedural fragility.

## The Three Stages

**Concrete.** Students manipulate physical objects — fraction tiles, paper strips, pattern blocks — to build intuitive understanding of part-whole relationships. At this stage the goal is *sense-making*, not notation. Empson & Levi (2011) show that children who spend adequate time here develop more flexible fraction reasoning later.

**Representational.** Students transition to visual models: bar diagrams, area models, and number lines. These bridge the gap between tactile experience and symbolic work. The number line is especially critical because it positions fractions as *numbers*, not just parts of shapes (Common Core 3.NF, 2010).

**Abstract.** Students use symbolic notation (a/b) and formal procedures (finding common denominators, simplifying). Premature abstraction — jumping straight to algorithms — correlates with the "add tops and bottoms" misconception (Lamon, 2012).

## Tutor Implications

When a student struggles with an abstract task, regress to the representational or concrete stage rather than re-explaining the algorithm. Probe whether the student can draw or describe what the fraction *means* before drilling the procedure. Equivalence and splitting concepts benefit especially from this regression strategy, as Van de Walle et al. (2018) emphasize that equivalence must be understood visually before it is computed symbolically.
