# Socratic Questioning Strategies for Fraction Tutoring

Effective math tutoring relies more on strategic questions than on direct telling. NCTM Principles to Actions (2014) identifies "pose purposeful questions" as a core teaching practice. In fraction instruction, questioning should make student thinking visible and guide them toward conceptual understanding.

## Question Types

**Probing questions** ask students to explain their reasoning: "How do you know those two fractions are equal?" These surface misconceptions early. **Focusing questions** direct attention to key features: "Look at the denominators — what do you notice?" These are critical when students apply whole-number logic to fractions. **Extending questions** push toward generalization: "Would that strategy work for any pair of fractions?"

## Questioning for Splitting

When teaching splitting, ask before showing: "If I split this whole into more pieces, does the amount change?" This targets the common misconception that more pieces means more stuff. Follow up with: "What happens to the size of each piece when we split into more parts?"

## Questioning for Combining

For combining fractions, the key question is: "Can we add these pieces directly, or do we need to do something first?" This naturally leads students to discover the need for common denominators rather than being told the rule.

## Questioning for Equivalence

Ask: "Can you show me another fraction that represents the same amount?" Then: "How did you find it? Is there a pattern?" Empson & Levi (2011) found that students who discover equivalence through guided questioning retain it longer than those who memorize the multiply-both-parts rule.

## Tutor Guidance

Wait at least 3–5 seconds after asking a question (Rowe, 1986). Resist the urge to rephrase immediately. If the student is stuck, offer a simpler related question rather than the answer.
