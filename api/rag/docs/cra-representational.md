# Representational Stage: Visual Models for Fractions

The representational (iconic) stage bridges concrete manipulation and abstract symbols. Students work with drawn or digital models that preserve the spatial reasoning of manipulatives while moving toward generalization.

## Core Visual Models

**Bar / tape models.** A rectangular bar partitioned into equal sections is the workhorse of fraction representation. It supports equivalence (subdivide sections to show 2/4 = 1/2), ordering (compare shaded areas side-by-side), and addition (join bars end-to-end). Cramer et al. (2002) found that students trained with bar models significantly outperformed peers on transfer tasks.

**Area models.** Circles and rectangles partitioned into regions. While circles are popular, research cautions that they make equal partitioning harder for odd denominators (Watanabe, 2002). Rectangles are generally preferable for instructional clarity.

**Number lines.** The Common Core (2010) emphasizes the number line starting in grade 3 because it frames fractions as *points on a continuum* rather than parts of shapes. This is essential for ordering and for understanding that fractions are numbers, not just "pieces of pizza." Siegler et al. (2011) found that number-line estimation accuracy is one of the strongest predictors of later fraction achievement.

## Tutor Implications

When a student cannot determine which of two fractions is larger, prompt a number-line or bar-model comparison before invoking cross-multiplication or common denominators. If a student claims 1/3 > 1/2, ask them to place both on a number line. The visual conflict between expectation and placement is a powerful catalyst for conceptual revision (Van de Walle et al., 2018).
