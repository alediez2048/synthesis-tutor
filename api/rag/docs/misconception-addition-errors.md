# Misconception: Adding Both Numerators and Denominators

## The Error

The single most prevalent fraction arithmetic error: students compute 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5 by adding numerators (1+1=2) and denominators (2+3=5). Mack (1995) documented this error appearing in the majority of students who learned the addition algorithm before developing conceptual understanding of fraction size.

## Why It Happens

The error is logical from a whole-number perspective — addition means "combine the numbers you see." Students who lack a mental model of what fraction addition *means* (combining quantities represented by different-sized unit pieces) default to the only operation they know: add everything in sight. Lamon (2012) emphasizes that this error is almost always a symptom of premature abstraction — the student was taught the procedure before the concept.

## Research-Based Interventions

**Bar model demonstration.** Draw a bar showing 1/2 shaded and another showing 1/3 shaded. Ask: "If we combine these amounts, do we get 2/5 of a bar?" The visual makes it obvious that the combined amount is more than 1/2, so 2/5 (which is less than 1/2) cannot be correct. This creates the cognitive conflict necessary for conceptual change (Vosniadou, 2013).

**Unit-fraction language.** Reframe the problem: "One-half is the same as three-sixths. One-third is the same as two-sixths. Now we have 3 sixths plus 2 sixths — how many sixths?" This leverages the student's correct understanding of like-unit addition (Empson & Levi, 2011).

**Estimation check.** Train students to always estimate first. "1/2 is about 0.5, 1/3 is about 0.33, so the answer should be about 0.83 — close to 5/6, not 2/5."

## Tutor Implications

When the `same_denominator` misconception tag fires during addition, the student is adding tops and bottoms. Avoid saying "you need a common denominator" as a first response — this is a rule without meaning. Instead, use the bar model or estimation strategy to help the student *see* why their answer is too small, then rebuild toward the common-denominator procedure.
