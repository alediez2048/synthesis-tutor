# Teaching Equivalent Fractions: Multiply/Divide Both Parts

Equivalence is the gateway concept in fraction understanding. Without it, students cannot compare, order, or add fractions with unlike denominators. Lamon (2012) calls equivalence "the heart of rational number reasoning."

## Conceptual Foundation First

Before introducing the multiply/divide procedure, students need to understand *why* it works. Use area models: show that 1/2 of a bar and 2/4 of the same bar cover the same area. Then ask: "What happened to the pieces? There are more of them, but each one is smaller. The total amount stayed the same." This mirrors Bruner's CRA progression — concrete understanding before abstract rules.

## The Multiply-Both-Parts Strategy

Once the concept is grounded, introduce the procedure: multiplying both numerator and denominator by the same nonzero number produces an equivalent fraction. Frame it as "making smaller pieces" — if you cut each piece in half (multiply denominator by 2), you get twice as many shaded pieces (multiply numerator by 2). CCSS 4.NF.1 formalizes this reasoning.

## The Divide-Both-Parts Strategy (Simplifying)

Simplifying is the reverse: dividing both parts by a common factor. Students often struggle here because they must identify a common factor — a prerequisite skill. Teach simplifying as "putting pieces back together" to maintain the concrete connection. Watch for the misconception that simplifying *changes* the fraction's value.

## Common Errors

Students may add the same number to both parts instead of multiplying (e.g., 1/3 becomes 2/4 by adding 1 to each). Explicitly contrast additive and multiplicative transformations with a counterexample.

## Tutor Guidance

Assess equivalence understanding before moving to addition. If a student cannot generate at least two equivalent fractions for a given fraction, they need more time in the explore phase before guided practice on addition.
