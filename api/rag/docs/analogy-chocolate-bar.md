# Chocolate Bar / Rectangular Area Model

The rectangular area model — often framed as a chocolate bar — is one of the most versatile fraction representations. Its grid structure makes equal partitioning straightforward and supports equivalence reasoning more naturally than circular models.

## Why Rectangles Work

Rectangles can be subdivided with simple horizontal or vertical cuts, producing visually equal pieces regardless of the denominator. Van de Walle et al. (2018) recommend rectangular models as a bridge between concrete and pictorial stages in the CRA (Concrete–Representational–Abstract) progression. A chocolate bar with 12 segments can represent halves, thirds, fourths, sixths, and twelfths — making it ideal for exploring equivalence.

## Teaching Splitting

Present a chocolate bar and ask: "How would you break this so two people each get a fair share?" Then three, then four. The physical grid constrains students to equal partitions, reducing the error of unequal splitting that plagues circular models. This aligns with CCSS 3.NF.1: understanding a fraction as the quantity formed by equal parts of a whole.

## Teaching Equivalence

Show that 2/8 and 1/4 of the same bar cover identical areas by shading. The grid makes it possible to *count* and *compare* without estimation. Empson & Levi (2011) found that area models help students develop multiplicative reasoning about equivalence earlier than set models do.

## Tutor Guidance

Introduce the chocolate bar alongside or shortly after the pizza model to build representational flexibility. Encourage students to predict how many segments to shade before doing it. When a student struggles with equivalence, return to the chocolate bar as a concrete anchor before working symbolically.
