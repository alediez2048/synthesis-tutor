# Common Core 4.NF: Equivalence, Ordering, and Comparing Fractions

The grade 4 NF domain extends fraction understanding to equivalence reasoning, systematic comparison, and the beginnings of fraction arithmetic with like denominators.

## Key Standards

**4.NF.A.1** — Explain why a fraction a/b is equivalent to (n x a)/(n x b) using visual models. Students must understand that multiplying numerator and denominator by the same number is the same as subdividing each piece into smaller equal parts. This is the formal expression of the splitting concept from grade 3.

**4.NF.A.2** — Compare two fractions with different numerators and different denominators by creating common denominators or numerators, or by comparing to a benchmark fraction (e.g., 1/2). The standards explicitly require justification with a visual model, not just a procedure.

**4.NF.B.3** — Add and subtract fractions with like denominators and mixed numbers. Students learn that adding fractions means combining unit-fraction copies: 2/5 + 1/5 = 3/5 because 2 copies plus 1 copy equals 3 copies of 1/5.

## Research Grounding

Empson & Levi (2011) demonstrate that students who develop equivalence through fair-sharing problems and visual partitioning transfer more flexibly to unlike-denominator problems in grade 5. The benchmark strategy (comparing to 0, 1/2, or 1) is strongly supported by Cramer et al. (2002) as a reasoning tool that persists into adult numeracy.

## Tutor Implications

When assessing ordering, ask students to explain *how they know* which fraction is larger. Accept benchmark reasoning ("3/8 is less than 1/2 because 4/8 would be 1/2") as valid — do not force common-denominator conversion if the student has a sound conceptual strategy. For simplifying, always connect back to visual equivalence before introducing the GCF procedure.
