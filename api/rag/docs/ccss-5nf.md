# Common Core 5.NF: Addition with Unlike Denominators

The grade 5 NF domain marks the transition to full fraction arithmetic. Addition and subtraction with unlike denominators is the central challenge, and it depends on all prior equivalence and unit-fraction understanding.

## Key Standards

**5.NF.A.1** — Add and subtract fractions with unlike denominators by replacing given fractions with equivalent fractions having a common denominator. The standard requires conceptual understanding of *why* a common denominator is needed: you cannot combine unlike unit fractions because they are different-sized pieces.

**5.NF.A.2** — Solve word problems involving addition and subtraction of fractions, using visual models and equations. Estimation with benchmarks is expected (e.g., recognizing that 1/2 + 3/7 should be close to 1, not close to 4/9).

**5.NF.B.4** — Multiply a fraction by a whole number or another fraction, interpreting multiplication as scaling. This reframes multiplication beyond repeated addition, which is critical for fractions.

## The Common Denominator Concept

Mack (1995) found that students who understand *why* a common denominator is needed — because you need same-sized pieces to combine — make far fewer errors than those who memorize the LCD algorithm. The instructional sequence should be: (1) show two fractions on a bar model with different partitions, (2) demonstrate that you cannot count mixed-size pieces, (3) subdivide both bars to create matching partitions, (4) *then* introduce the symbolic algorithm.

## Tutor Implications

When a student adds numerators and denominators (e.g., 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5), this is the single most common fraction error. Do not just say "that's wrong." Instead, ask: "If you ate half a pizza and then a third of a pizza, did you eat only 2/5 of a pizza? Does that seem like less than a half?" Use estimation and visual models to create cognitive conflict before re-teaching the procedure.
