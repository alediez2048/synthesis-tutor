# Misconception: Applying Whole Number Rules to Fractions

## The Error

Students treat fraction components as independent whole numbers, leading to errors like: "2/3 > 3/5 because 2 + 3 = 5 and 3 + 5 = 8, and 5 < 8 so 2/3 is simpler, so it is bigger." Or they may believe 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5 by adding tops and bottoms separately. Ni & Zhou (2005) call this the *whole number bias* — the tendency to apply additive, counting-based reasoning to a fundamentally multiplicative system.

## Why It Happens

Fractions are the first number system students encounter where the rules of whole-number arithmetic break. Stafylidou & Vosniadou (2004) describe this as a *framework theory* conflict: students' existing mental model of "number" was built entirely around counting, and fractions require a fundamental restructuring. This is not a simple error — it is a conceptual transition that takes sustained instruction to complete.

## Research-Based Interventions

**Fair-sharing problems.** Empson & Levi (2011) show that situating fractions in sharing contexts ("Share 3 cookies among 4 children") leverages children's intuitive understanding of division and produces fraction reasoning that avoids whole-number traps.

**Estimation before computation.** Before any symbolic operation, ask: "About how big is the answer?" A student who estimates 1/2 + 1/3 as "a bit less than 1" will immediately see that 2/5 cannot be right, because 2/5 is less than 1/2 alone.

**Explicit contrast.** Van de Walle et al. (2018) recommend directly comparing how operations work differently: "With whole numbers, bigger digits mean more. With fractions, a bigger denominator means smaller pieces."

## Tutor Implications

When the `used_whole_number` tag fires, the student is likely applying counting logic to fractions. Ground your response in estimation or visual comparison rather than restating the algorithm. Help the student notice the conflict between their answer and their intuition.
