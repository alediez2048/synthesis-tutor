# Concrete Stage: Physical Manipulatives for Fractions

The concrete stage is where fraction understanding begins. Research consistently shows that extended concrete experience produces more durable learning than early symbolic instruction (Cramer et al., 2002; Empson & Levi, 2011).

## Key Manipulatives

**Fraction tiles / fraction strips.** Pre-partitioned pieces (halves, thirds, fourths, etc.) let students physically compare sizes, discover equivalences, and combine pieces. When a student places two 1/4 tiles next to one 1/2 tile and sees they match, splitting and combining become tangible facts rather than memorized rules.

**Paper folding.** Folding a strip into equal parts and shading portions is one of the most effective activities for building the splitting concept (Lamon, 2012). It forces attention to equal partitioning — a prerequisite for all fraction understanding.

**Pattern blocks.** Using hexagons, trapezoids, and triangles, students explore part-whole relationships where the whole can change, preparing them for later work with different denominators.

## Tutor Implications

In a digital tutoring context, replicate concrete experiences through interactive visual manipulatives that students drag, split, and combine. When a student makes an error in combining fractions (e.g., 1/3 + 1/3 = 2/6), prompt them to use the virtual tiles: "If you put two 1/3 pieces together, how much of the bar is covered?" This grounds the correction in physical intuition rather than rule correction. Bruner's (1966) framework insists that the enactive layer must be solid before advancing; rushing past it is the most common instructional mistake in fraction teaching.
