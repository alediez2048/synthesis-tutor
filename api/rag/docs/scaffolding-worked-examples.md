# Worked Examples and Fading for Fraction Problems

The worked example effect (Sweller & Cooper, 1985) shows that studying completed solutions produces better learning than solving equivalent problems, especially for novices. In fraction instruction, this means presenting fully solved equivalence and addition problems before asking students to solve their own.

## Structure of a Worked Example

A strong worked example for fractions has three layers: (1) the problem statement, (2) each procedural step with its rationale, and (3) a summary connecting the steps to the underlying concept. For addition: show that 1/3 + 1/6 requires a common denominator, explain *why* (the pieces must be the same size to combine), show the conversion 1/3 = 2/6, then combine to get 3/6 and simplify to 1/2.

## Fading Strategy

Renkl et al. (2002) demonstrated that backward fading — gradually removing steps from worked examples — outperforms abrupt transitions. Start with a complete solution. Next, blank out the final step and ask the student to finish. Then blank the last two steps, and so on. This aligns with the NCTM (2014) recommendation to build procedural fluency from conceptual understanding.

## Application to Equivalence

For equivalence problems, fade the multiplication/division step first, since students can often verify the result visually even before they can generate the procedure. Van de Walle et al. (2018) recommend pairing each symbolic worked example with a visual model so the student connects procedure to meaning.

## Tutor Guidance

Present at most two fully worked examples before beginning to fade. Monitor whether the student reads the rationale or skips to answers — redirect attention to the "why" when needed.
