# Abstract Stage: Symbolic Notation and Formal Procedures

The abstract stage is where students work with fraction symbols (a/b), apply algorithms (finding common denominators, simplifying), and reason without visual supports. This stage should only be reached after solid concrete and representational foundations.

## Key Symbolic Skills

**Reading and writing fractions.** Students must understand that a/b means "a parts out of b equal parts" — not two separate whole numbers. Misreading the vinculum (fraction bar) as a separator rather than a division operation is a documented source of error (Lamon, 2012).

**Equivalence procedures.** Multiplying numerator and denominator by the same factor (n/n = 1) is the core equivalence algorithm. Students who learned equivalence visually first (bar subdivisions, number-line overlays) can explain *why* the procedure works, not just execute it (Empson & Levi, 2011).

**Simplifying.** Dividing numerator and denominator by a common factor. Students often resist simplifying because they do not trust that 4/8 and 1/2 name the same amount. Refer back to representational models to rebuild that trust.

**Formal addition/subtraction.** Finding a common denominator, converting, then combining numerators. The most common error — adding both numerators and denominators — arises when students learn the algorithm without representational grounding (Mack, 1995).

## Tutor Implications

When a student makes a procedural error at the abstract level, do not simply correct the symbol manipulation. Diagnose whether the underlying concept is intact: ask the student to draw or explain what the operation means. If they cannot, regress to the representational stage. Van de Walle et al. (2018) stress that premature formalization produces brittle knowledge that collapses under novel problem types.
