# Rubric for Fraction Understanding Levels

Assessing fraction knowledge requires more than checking answers. This rubric, informed by Lamon (2012) and the CCSS progression documents, distinguishes three levels of understanding: recognition, construction, and generalization.

## Level 1: Recognition

The student can identify fractions in visual models and match symbolic representations to pictures. They recognize that 1/2 of a shaded bar means "one of two equal parts." At this level, students may not yet generate fractions independently. Assessment items: select the picture that shows 3/4; identify the fraction represented by a shaded region. Errors at this level often involve confusing numerator and denominator roles.

## Level 2: Construction

The student can create equivalent fractions, partition models to show given fractions, and perform addition with like denominators. They understand *why* common denominators are needed, even if execution is inconsistent. Assessment items: shade a model to show 2/3; find a fraction equal to 3/6; compute 2/5 + 1/5. Errors at this level tend to be procedural — off_by_one in counting parts, or forgetting to simplify.

## Level 3: Generalization

The student flexibly applies fraction concepts to novel problems. They can add fractions with unlike denominators, explain equivalence in their own words, and self-correct errors. They transfer across representations (area model, number line, symbols). Assessment items: compute 1/3 + 1/4 and explain your method; determine whether 3/8 or 2/5 is larger and justify your answer.

## Using the Rubric

NCTM Principles to Actions (2014) recommends using evidence of student thinking — not just final answers — to determine understanding level. Ask students to explain their reasoning. A correct answer with no explanation may indicate memorization, not understanding.

## Tutor Guidance

Adjust the phase and scaffolding level based on the student's rubric level. Level 1 students belong in intro/explore; Level 2 in guided; Level 3 in assess/complete. Regression is normal — a student may perform at Level 3 for equivalence but Level 1 for addition.
