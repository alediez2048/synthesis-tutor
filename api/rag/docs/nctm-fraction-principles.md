# NCTM Principles to Actions: Fraction-Specific Teaching Practices

NCTM's *Principles to Actions* (2014) identifies eight Mathematics Teaching Practices. Several are especially relevant to fraction instruction and should shape how the AI tutor interacts with students.

## Relevant Teaching Practices

**Implement tasks that promote reasoning and problem solving.** Fraction tasks should require thinking, not just executing steps. Instead of "Simplify 6/8," ask "Find a fraction equal to 6/8 that uses smaller numbers, and explain how you know they are equal." This shifts the cognitive demand from procedural to conceptual (Smith & Stein, 1998).

**Use and connect mathematical representations.** Students should work across representations: physical models, drawings, number lines, and symbols. NCTM (2014) stresses that connections *between* representations build deeper understanding than any single representation alone. When teaching equivalence, link the bar model subdivision to the symbolic multiplication explicitly.

**Facilitate meaningful mathematical discourse.** Even in a one-on-one tutoring context, ask students to explain their reasoning. Prompts like "How do you know?" and "Can you show me another way?" push students beyond answer-getting toward sense-making (Empson & Levi, 2011).

**Build procedural fluency from conceptual understanding.** This is the CRA principle restated as policy. Algorithms for finding common denominators, simplifying, and combining fractions must grow out of visual and conceptual work — not precede it. Hiebert & Grouws (2007) confirm that instruction emphasizing conceptual foundations produces equal or better procedural fluency compared to procedure-first teaching.

## Tutor Implications

When providing feedback, avoid purely evaluative responses ("Correct!" / "Try again."). Instead, use *descriptive* feedback that references the mathematics: "You found that 2/6 = 1/3 by dividing both by 2 — that works because you are splitting each group in half, which does not change the amount." This models the discourse practices NCTM recommends.
